Introduction

My research question is ultimately what the demographic shifts in the San Gabriel Valley from the last round of redistricting can reveal about Asian voting behavior through the most recent elections in 2022. It analyzes Asian voting behavior by looking at a specific suburban enclave in Los Angeles. California District 38, which previously had a smaller proportion of Asian residents, now includes a region known as the East San Gabriel Valley, which holds a majority East and Southeast Asian population. Thus, I want to analyze the impact of this demogaphic shift and the relationship between being Asian and voting based on descriptive representation versus partisan representation. Despite being a typically blue demographic, there have been several elections in which a Republican Asian candidate has won. My hypothesis is that Asians voters generally value descriptive representation over partisan representation when these identities come into conflict. My research aims to understand this contradictory phenomenon by trying to study Asian voting patterns and behaviors, which ultimately reveal the priorities and values of Asian voters when casting ballots.

Data

My data is taken from the California Statewide Database and is specifically filtered to the election data from the current California District 38, including the regions that were redrawn into CA-38, which is essentially the the aforementioned East SGV. The data was collected through voters’ Statement of Vote and the Statement of Registration in conjunction with the geography files from all 58 counties for each election. The purpose of the statewide database is to have an established place with election data that can be compared overtime.

Because my data is raw, there is no explicit treatment, control, or variable that was trying to be studied during data collection. However, in the context of my research, the key independent variables are the population and propotion of Asians in CA-38 and the key dependent variable are the number and proportion of votes casted for a particular candidate in a particular election. add design type and treatment (demographic shift, basically)

The last two figures are linear regressions displaying the relationship between the proportion of Asians in CA-38 and the proportion of votes cast for a given party (either Democrat or Republican) in congressional elections pre- and post-redistricting. Figure 3 illustrates this relationship in the 2020 congressional election between Linda Sánchez and Michael Tolar, both non-Asian Democrats. Here, as the proportion of Asian voters increases, the proportion of votes for both Democrats and Republicans decrease minimally. There is no evident contrast in the number of votes cast for one party versus another.

However, when compared to Figure 4, which displays a regression of the results of the 2022 congressional election between Linda Sánchez and Eric Ching, an Asian Republican, the slopes for the proportion of votes cast to each party steepen. This regression demonstrates evidence of higher crossover voting in the 2022 CA-38 congressional election compared to the 2020 congressional election in which there was no Asian candidate present. Here, when the proportion of Asians inreases, the proportion of votes for the Democratic candidate (Linda Sánchez) decreases while the proportion of votes for the Republican candidate (Eric Ching) increases. The regressions in Figure 3 show a weaker relationship between the proportion of Asians in a given area versus the proportion of votes given to a certain party compared to the regressions in Figure 4. This comparison indicates that Asian populations generally do not have strong associations with Republicans but in elections where the Republican candidate holds an Asian identity, that association may strengthen.
